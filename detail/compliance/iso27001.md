# The Mathematics of ISO 27001 — Risk Quantification, Control Effectiveness, and ISMS Optimization

> *An Information Security Management System is fundamentally a feedback control system: risk assessment quantifies threat likelihood and impact using probability distributions, control effectiveness is measured through coverage ratios and failure rates, audit sampling follows statistical inference, and the PDCA cycle can be modeled as a convergent optimization process that minimizes residual risk within resource constraints.*

---

## 1. Risk Assessment Quantification (Probability Theory)
### The Problem
ISO 27001 Clause 6.1.2 requires a risk assessment methodology that identifies, analyzes, and evaluates information security risks. A quantitative approach uses probability distributions to model threat likelihood and impact, producing risk scores that can be compared, prioritized, and tracked over time.

### The Formula
Risk as the expected loss for asset $a$ under threat $t$ exploiting vulnerability $v$:

$$R(a, t, v) = P(t) \times P(v \mid t) \times I(a)$$

where $P(t)$ is the probability of threat occurrence, $P(v \mid t)$ is the probability the threat exploits the vulnerability, and $I(a)$ is the impact on the asset.

Total organizational risk:

$$R_{total} = \sum_{a \in A} \sum_{t \in T} \sum_{v \in V_a} P(t) \cdot P(v \mid t) \cdot I(a)$$

After applying control $c$ with effectiveness $e_c \in [0, 1]$:

$$R_{residual}(a, t, v, c) = P(t) \times P(v \mid t) \times (1 - e_c) \times I(a)$$

Risk appetite threshold $\theta$: risk is acceptable when $R_{residual} \leq \theta$.

### Worked Examples
**Example**: A company assesses the risk of an SQL injection attack on its customer database.

- Asset value: $I = 500{,}000$ (financial impact of data breach)
- Threat probability: $P(t) = 0.3$ per year (web application attacks)
- Vulnerability probability: $P(v|t) = 0.4$ (unvalidated inputs)

$$R_{inherent} = 0.3 \times 0.4 \times 500{,}000 = \$60{,}000 \text{ per year}$$

After implementing a WAF ($e_1 = 0.7$) and parameterized queries ($e_2 = 0.95$):

Combined effectiveness: $e_{combined} = 1 - (1 - 0.7)(1 - 0.95) = 1 - 0.015 = 0.985$

$$R_{residual} = 0.3 \times 0.4 \times (1 - 0.985) \times 500{,}000 = \$900 \text{ per year}$$

If $\theta = \$5{,}000$, the residual risk of $\$900$ is acceptable.

## 2. Control Effectiveness Measurement (Statistical Testing)
### The Problem
Clause 9.1 requires monitoring, measurement, analysis, and evaluation of ISMS performance. Control effectiveness must be quantified to demonstrate that the ISMS achieves its objectives and to identify controls requiring improvement.

### The Formula
Control effectiveness as a ratio of detected-and-prevented incidents to total threats:

$$E_c = \frac{\text{Threats detected and mitigated}}{\text{Total threats encountered}} = \frac{D}{D + M}$$

where $D$ is detected threats and $M$ is missed threats.

Confidence interval for effectiveness based on $n$ observations:

$$E_c \pm z_{\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{E_c(1 - E_c)}{n}}$$

For a portfolio of $k$ controls protecting the same asset, combined effectiveness:

$$E_{combined} = 1 - \prod_{i=1}^{k} (1 - E_i)$$

Control maturity can be modeled as a learning curve:

$$E_c(t) = E_{max} \left(1 - e^{-\lambda t}\right)$$

where $E_{max}$ is the theoretical maximum effectiveness and $\lambda$ is the maturity rate.

### Worked Examples
**Example**: An organization measures its email security control over 12 months.

- Phishing emails received: 2,400
- Blocked by spam filter: 2,100
- Caught by user reporting: 180
- Successful phishing (missed): 120

$$E_{filter} = \frac{2{,}100}{2{,}400} = 0.875$$

$$E_{combined} = \frac{2{,}100 + 180}{2{,}400} = 0.950$$

95% confidence interval for combined effectiveness ($n = 2{,}400$):

$$0.950 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.950 \times 0.050}{2{,}400}} = 0.950 \pm 0.0087$$

$$CI = [0.941, 0.959]$$

The control is effective at 95.0% (95% CI: 94.1%-95.9%).

## 3. Audit Sampling Theory (Statistical Inference)
### The Problem
Both internal audits (Clause 9.2) and certification audits sample evidence from the ISMS. The sample size must be large enough to provide confidence that the population of controls is conformant.

### The Formula
For attribute sampling (control is conformant or not), the required sample size:

$$n = \frac{z^2 \cdot p(1-p)}{e^2}$$

where $z$ is the z-score for desired confidence, $p$ is the expected nonconformity rate, and $e$ is the tolerable error.

For a finite population of $N$ controls:

$$n_{adjusted} = \frac{n}{1 + \frac{n - 1}{N}}$$

The probability of detecting at least one nonconformity when the true rate is $p$ with sample size $n$:

$$P(\text{detect} \geq 1) = 1 - (1 - p)^n$$

### Worked Examples
**Example**: An auditor must sample from 93 Annex A controls. Expected nonconformity rate is 5%, desired confidence is 95% ($z = 1.96$), tolerable error is 5%.

$$n = \frac{1.96^2 \times 0.05 \times 0.95}{0.05^2} = \frac{3.8416 \times 0.0475}{0.0025} = \frac{0.1825}{0.0025} = 73.0$$

Adjusted for finite population:

$$n_{adjusted} = \frac{73.0}{1 + \frac{72.0}{93}} = \frac{73.0}{1.774} = 41.1 \approx 42$$

The auditor must sample at least 42 of the 93 controls.

Probability of detecting a nonconformity if the true rate is 10%:

$$P(\text{detect} \geq 1) = 1 - (1 - 0.10)^{42} = 1 - 0.90^{42} = 1 - 0.0115 = 0.9885$$

98.9% probability of detecting at least one nonconformity at a true 10% rate.

## 4. PDCA Convergence (Control Theory)
### The Problem
The PDCA cycle is a feedback loop that should drive residual risk downward over time. Control theory models this as a discrete-time system whose convergence depends on the gain and feedback characteristics of each phase.

### The Formula
Residual risk at PDCA iteration $k$:

$$R_k = R_{k-1} \cdot (1 - G_k)$$

where $G_k$ is the risk reduction gain at iteration $k$, dependent on:

$$G_k = \alpha \cdot A_k \cdot F_k$$

- $\alpha$: organizational learning rate
- $A_k$: audit coverage ratio at iteration $k$
- $F_k$: fraction of findings remediated

The system converges when $0 < G_k < 1$ for all $k$. After $n$ cycles:

$$R_n = R_0 \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{n} (1 - G_k)$$

For constant gain $G$:

$$R_n = R_0 \cdot (1 - G)^n$$

Time to reach target risk $R_{target}$:

$$n^* = \frac{\ln(R_{target} / R_0)}{\ln(1 - G)}$$

### Worked Examples
**Example**: An organization starts with $R_0 = 100$ (normalized risk score). Target: $R_{target} = 10$.

With consistent PDCA execution: $\alpha = 0.8$, $A = 0.9$, $F = 0.85$:

$$G = 0.8 \times 0.9 \times 0.85 = 0.612$$

$$n^* = \frac{\ln(10/100)}{\ln(1 - 0.612)} = \frac{-2.303}{-0.947} = 2.43$$

About 2.5 PDCA cycles (approximately 2.5 years at annual cycles) to reach the target.

After 3 full cycles:

$$R_3 = 100 \times (1 - 0.612)^3 = 100 \times 0.388^3 = 100 \times 0.0585 = 5.85$$

The organization exceeds its target by cycle 3.

## 5. Information Asset Valuation (Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis)
### The Problem
Clause 8.1 of ISO 27002 requires an inventory of assets, and risk assessment requires valuing them. When assets have multiple value dimensions (confidentiality, integrity, availability), multi-criteria methods aggregate partial valuations into a single risk-usable score.

### The Formula
Asset value using weighted sum of CIA impact dimensions:

$$V(a) = w_C \cdot C(a) + w_I \cdot I(a) + w_A \cdot A(a)$$

where $w_C + w_I + w_A = 1$ and $C, I, A$ are impact scores for each dimension.

For the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) to determine weights:

$$w_i = \frac{\left(\prod_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij}\right)^{1/n}}{\sum_{k=1}^{n}\left(\prod_{j=1}^{n} a_{kj}\right)^{1/n}}$$

where $a_{ij}$ is the pairwise comparison of criterion $i$ vs $j$.

Consistency ratio: $CR = CI / RI$ where $CI = (\lambda_{max} - n)/(n-1)$ and $CR < 0.1$ indicates consistent judgments.

### Worked Examples
**Example**: An organization values its customer database across CIA dimensions. Pairwise comparisons:

| | C | I | A |
|---|---|---|---|
| C | 1 | 3 | 5 |
| I | 1/3 | 1 | 3 |
| A | 1/5 | 1/3 | 1 |

Geometric means: $C: (1 \times 3 \times 5)^{1/3} = 2.466$, $I: (0.333 \times 1 \times 3)^{1/3} = 1.0$, $A: (0.2 \times 0.333 \times 1)^{1/3} = 0.405$

Sum: $2.466 + 1.0 + 0.405 = 3.871$

Weights: $w_C = 0.637$, $w_I = 0.258$, $w_A = 0.105$

With impact scores $C = 5$, $I = 4$, $A = 3$:

$$V = 0.637 \times 5 + 0.258 \times 4 + 0.105 \times 3 = 3.185 + 1.032 + 0.315 = 4.53$$

The database scores 4.53 out of 5, confirming it as a high-value asset requiring extensive controls.
